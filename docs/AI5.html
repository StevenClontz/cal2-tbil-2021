(b)

  2. \(500\cdot 15=7500\) kg.

  3. \(500\cdot 15\cdot 5=37500\) kg.

Is this estimate the actual mass of this piece?

Fact 2.5.19.

Consider a solid where the cross section of the solid at \(x=x_i\) has area \(A(x_i)\text{,}\) and the density when \(x=x_i\) is \(\delta(x_i)\text{.}\) Suppose the interval \([a,b]\) represented the \(x\) values of this solid. If one sliced the solid into \(n\) pieces of width \(\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}\text{,}\) then one can approximate the mass of the solid by

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n \delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x. \end{equation*}

We can then find actual mass by taking the limit as \(n\to\infty\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(\sum_{i=1}^n \delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x\right)=\int_a^b \delta(x)A(x) dx. \end{equation*}

Fact 2.5.31.

Consider a solid where the cross section of the solid at \(x=x_i\) has area \(A(x_i)\text{,}\) and the density when \(x=x_i\) is \(\delta(x_i)\text{.}\) Suppose the interval \([a,b]\) represented the \(x\) values of this solid. Since each slice has approximated mass \(\delta(x_i)A(x_i)\delta(x_i)\text{,}\) we can approximate the center of mass by taking the weighted “average” of the \(x_i\)-values weighted by the associated mass:

\begin{equation*} \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x}{\sum_{i=1}^n \delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x}. \end{equation*}

We can then find actual center of mass by taking the limit as \(n\to\infty\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x}{\sum_{i=1}^n \delta(x_i)A(x_i)\Delta x}\right)=\frac{\int_a^b x\delta(x)A(x)dx}{\int_a^b \delta(x)A(x)dx}=\frac{\int_a^b x\delta(x)A(x)dx}{\text{The Total Mass}}. \end{equation*}
