Activity 3.3.12.

Suppose that you were handed positive sequences \(\{a_n\}, \{b_n\}\text{.}\) For the first few values \(a_n\geq b_n\text{,}\) but after that what happens is unclear until \(n=100\text{.}\) Then for any \(n\geq 100\) we have that \(a_n \leq b_n\text{.}\)

(a)

How might we best utilize Fact 3.3.11 to determine the convergence of \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\) or \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n\text{?}\)

  1. Since \(a_n\) is sometimes greater than, and sometimes less than \(b_n\text{,}\) there is no way to utilize Fact 3.3.11.

  2. Since initially, we have \(b_n\leq a_n\text{,}\) we can utilize Fact 3.3.11 by assuming \(a_n\geq b_n\text{.}\)

  3. Since we can rewrite \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n=\sum_{n=0}^{99} a_n+\sum_{n=100}^\infty a_n\) and \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n=\sum_{n=0}^{99} b_n+\sum_{n=100}^\infty b_n\) and \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{99} a_n, \sum_{n=0}^{99} b_n\) are necessarily finite, we can compare \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=100}^\infty a_n, \sum_{n=100}^\infty b_n\) with Fact 3.3.11.
